## Impulse and the Momentum Principle

### Defining Impulse and Momentum

The momentum of an object is defined as the product of its mass $m$ and
its velocity $v$. Newton's Second Law provides a direct relationship
between force and the change in momentum over time:
$$\vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{P}}{dt}$$

Impulse ($\vec{I}$) is a vector quantity
defined as the integral of force as a function of time over a specific interval:
$$\vec{I} = \int_{t_{initial}}^{t_{final}} \vec{F}(t) dt$$
By applying Newton's Second Law to this definition, we arrive at the Momentum
Principle: impulse causes momentum to change. The impulse is equal to
the final momentum minus the initial momentum:
$$\vec{I} = \vec{P}_{final} - \vec{P}_{initial}$$
This is a vector equation, meaning it holds true for each individual
component ($x, y,$ and $z$) of the motion.

### Units of Measurement

The SI units for both impulse and momentum are the same:
kilogram-meters per second $kg \cdot m/s$

## Application to Collisions and Average Force

## Collision Dynamics

During a collision, such as a ball bouncing off the ground, the object
undergoes deformation. In a real-world scenario, this deformation is not
completely elastic; some energy is transformed into molecular motion, which
becomes thermal energy radiated into the environment.

### Calculating Average Normal Force

The momentum principle is used to determine the average force exerted during a
collision. For a ball of mass $m$ dropping from a height $h_i$ and rebounding to
$h_f$:

* Velocities: Kinematics determines the velocity before impact
$v_{before} = \sqrt{2gh_i}$ and after impact $v_{after} = \sqrt{2gh_f}$.
* Force Equation: The integral of the forces (Normal force $N$ minus gravitational
  force $mg$) over the collision time $\Delta t$ equals the change in momentum.
* The Result: The average normal force $N_{avg}$ can be expressed as:
  $$N_{avg} =  \frac{m(v_{after} + v_{before})}{\Delta t} + mg$$
  This equation demonstrates that a shorter collision time results in a larger
  average force, while a longer collision time reduces it.

## Systems of Particles

### Total Momentum of a System

For a system containing n particles, the total momentum $\vec{P}_{sys}$ is the
vector sum of the individual momenta of all particles:
$$\vec{P}_{sys} = \sum_{j=1}^{n} m_j \vec{v}_j$$

### Internal vs. External Forces

Forces acting on particles within a system are categorized into two types:

* External Forces $\vec{F}_{ext}$: Interactions between a particle inside the
  system and an object outside the system (e.g., gravity from a planet outside
  the defined system).
* Internal Forces $\vec{F}_{int}$: Interactions between two particles both
  located within the system.

According to Newton's Third Law, internal forces occur in action-reaction pairs
that are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. Consequently, when
summing the forces for the entire system, all internal forces cancel each other
out $\sum \vec{F}_{int} = 0$.

### Newton's Second Law for a System

Because internal forces cancel, the total force acting on a system is
effectively just the sum of the external forces. Therefore, the change in the
total momentum of a system is caused solely by external forces:
$$\sum \vec{F}_{ext} = \frac{d\vec{P}_{sys}}{dt}$$

## Conservation of Momentum

### Conditions for Conservation

The momentum of a system is constant (conserved) between an initial and final
time if the total external impulse is zero. This occurs when:

* The total external force is zero.
* The time interval is so small that the impulse from external forces (like
  friction) is negligible.

### Multi-Dimensional Considerations

Momentum conservation is a vector principle. It is possible for momentum to be
conserved in one dimension (e.g., the x-direction) but not in another (e.g., the
y-direction) if there is a net external force acting only in that specific
direction.

## Momentum Diagrams

Momentum diagrams are a methodological tool used to represent collisions. The
process involves:

1. Choosing the system and a reference frame (e.g., the ground frame).
2. Identifying the initial and final states.
3. Representing velocities as vectors on a diagram.
4. Applying the momentum principle to set the initial momentum equal to the
   final momentum (if external forces are zero).
5. Choosing a coordinate system to resolve the vector equation into components.

## The Center of Mass (CM)

### Definition and Conceptual Meaning

The center of mass is the unique point in an object or system where the mass-
weighted position vectors sum to zero.

* For Discrete Particles: The position of the center of mass $\vec{R}_{cm}$
  relative to an origin is:
  $$\vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{1}{M_{total}} \sum_{j=1}^{n} m_j \vec{r}_j$$
* For Continuous Objects: The sum is replaced by an integral over the body:
  $$\vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{1}{M_{total}} \int \vec{r} dm$$

### Calculating the Center of Mass for a Uniform Rod

For a uniform rod of length L, the calculation involves introducing a linear
mass density $\lambda = \frac{M}{L}$ and an integration variable $x'$:

* $dm = \lambda dx'$
* Integrating from 0 to L results in a center of mass at $\frac{L}{2}$, exactly in the middle of the rod.

### Center of Mass Dynamics

The velocity and acceleration of the center of mass are vital for understanding
system motion:

* Velocity $\vec{V}_{cm}$: The sum of the individual momenta divided by the
  total mass. The velocity of the CM is independent of the choice of a fixed
  origin point.
* Acceleration $\vec{A}_{cm}$: The total external force divided by the total
  mass of the system.

### Reduction to a Point Particle

The dynamics of a complex system of particles can be reduced to the
translational motion of a single point particle. By placing the total mass of
the system $M_{total}$ at the center of mass and applying all external forces to
that point, we can describe the motion of the entire system using the equation:

$$\sum \vec{F}_{ext} = M_{total} \vec{A}_{cm}$$

This reduction allows physicists to
ignore complicated internal interactions when analyzing the overall translation
of a system through space.

Glossary of Key Terms

| Term                | Definition                                                                                                                                |
|---------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Center of Mass      | The unique point in a system where the sum of mass-weighted position vectors relative to that point is zero.                              |
| External Force      | A force exerted on a particle within a system by an object that is not part of that system.                                               |
| Impulse             | A vector quantity defined as the integral of force over a time interval; it is equal to the change in momentum.                           |
| Internal Force      | A force exerted by one particle within a system on another particle within the same system.                                               |
| Linear Mass Density | The mass per unit length of a continuous one-dimensional object, often denoted as M/L.                                                    |
| Momentum            | The product of an object's mass and its velocity (\vec{p}=m\vec{v}).                                                                      |
| Momentum Diagram    | A visual representation of a system's initial and final states using velocity vectors to analyze collisions.                              |
| Momentum Principle  | The rule stating that the impulse applied to an object or system is equal to its change in momentum.                                      |
| Newton's Second Law | The law stating that the force acting on an object is equal to the time rate of change of its momentum.                                   |
| Newton's Third Law  | The law stating that for every internal force exerted on one particle, there is an equal and opposite reaction force on another particle. |
| Reference Frame     | A coordinate system used to measure the positions and velocities of objects within a system.                                              |
